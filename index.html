Climate Change

GESTSOC

Introduction.

Climate change is a change in the pattern of weather, oceans, land surfaces and ice sheets, occurring over decades or longer. Air pollution is one of the biggest threats for the environment and affects everyone: humans, animals, crops, cities, forests, and aquatic ecosystems.

Currently, climate change is happening and its effects are being felt throughout the world. As of now, the damage is only getting worse. Severe storms, heat waves, and accelerated melting of glaciers and ice sheets are examples of the effects global warming can cause. Today, policymakers and air quality managers rely on cutting-edge science to establish regulations and make management decisions to reduce and control air pollution with cost-effective approaches.

Issues.

Air pollution could be the effect of numerous things. Malpractice of people, littering, corporate irresponsibility, lack of governance, but it all boils down to a single point. It is not natural. Pollution is the consequence of humanity’s carelessness and its impact to society is far more grave than we realize. In the eyes of the privileged, it may seem like pollution is a shallow topic to swallow, considering how saturated it is already. However, for the poor, pollution may just be the cause of their death. There is nothing natural about pollution. As most wastes found in polluted sites cannot degrade naturally. These are the plastics, electronic components, wet wipes, rubber-nearly all common everyday items are the cause of pollution.

Green capitalism is a coined term used to describe the actions of corporations taking advantage of advocacies for profit. Issues surrounding the environment have always been treated as something shallow as compared to its contemporaries. This is due to people jumping on shallow acts of activism to either acquire clout or simply present themselves as socially aware of such issues. Companies have been doing this tactic since the 90’s and people still have not learned to channel their care toward something more productive. The bottom line is that corporations do not care and even if they do, the gross actions they do still outweigh the few months of pandering they do for advocacy groups.

Timeline.

  • 13th Century

    During the late 13th century, King Edward I of England threatened harsh penalties to Londoners if they refused to stop burning sea-coal, in an effort to reduce air pollution. These regulations had little effect, even in the hands of subsequent leaders.

  • 19th Century

    Early part of the 19th century, the Industrial Revolution was in full swing and coal became a largely used source of fuel for the machines of this time. This resulted in growing amounts of smog and soot which negatively affected the health of many residents of these growing urban areas, as well as contributing to the increase of air pollution.

  • 1980s

    By the 1980’s, computer models showed that a doubling of CO2 could warm the Earth by 2 degrees Celsius within the next century.

  • 1997

    By 1997, the Kyoto Protocol was enacted as the first global agreement to reduce greenhouse gases.

  • 2050

    A deadline was set by the UN Climate Action Summit to achieve net zero emissions by 2050.

  • 1960s

    The "Tragedy of the Commons", an essay published in 1968 in Science by Garrett Hardin, claimed the inevitability of malthusian catastrophe due to liberal or democratic government's policies to leave family size matters to the family, and enabling the welfare state to willingly care for potential human overpopulation.

  • 1982

    By 1982, John Baden created PERC, now known as the Property and Environment Research Center, which would uphold the mission of “improving environment quality through property rights and markets”.

  • 1990s

    International emissions trading was significantly popularized in the 1990’s when the US adopted the Kyoto Protocol.

Figures.

King Edward I.

King Edward I set up the world's first air pollution commission and banned the burning of coal. He also imposed punishments when people were caught burning coal as it was harming the air around his kingdom. This was the start of Britian's air problem.

U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) dedicated much of its effort to regulating air pollutants. Of all the major environmental rules promulgated from 2009 to 2012, more than 60 percent were adopted under just one statute, the Clean Air Act (CAA)

  • Solutions.

  • Solution 4

    Air Pollution

    Air Monitoring

    Nearly all countries have some form of air-monitoring system. It is basically the process of checking the air quality index of each geographical area to determine if it is safe and use this information to try to alleviate said issues. What science-oriented air monitoring means is that it is the systematic, long-term assessment of pollutant levels by measuring the quantity and types of certain pollutants in the surrounding, outdoor air with the next step in mind. Meaning, the data the monitoring system mines will be logged in the database of its respective local and the data will be used in identifying patterns and threats with the air we breathe currently.

  • Solution 3

    Air Pollution

    Alternative Energy

    Biofuels of plant origin proved to be controversial due to competition for food production, fertile land and expensive production processes. As a secondary alternative, microalgae such as Scenedesmus obliquus, Chlorella vulgaris and Nannochloropsis sp. were found to be suitable candidates for liquid biofuels production. This is a brief description of their proposal to combat climate change. An accessible alternative to competitive biomass.

  • Solution 2

    Green Capitalism

    Raw Material Management

    Changing the raw materials that capitalism uses for production to make sure it is environmentally sustainable. This is supported by Rocha, de Souza, & Prentice (2018) as their study on alternative packaging cut down costs while removing the strain on natural resources. Mazur (2018) also shares the sentiment of changing green capitalism by proposing to change the transport system of products, since emission is a unsustainable issue made by deliveries, transfers, and transport-related activities needing vehicles, the use of electric cars cut down those harmful emissions and can radically increase air quality and increase efficiency.

  • Solution 1

    Green Capitalism

    Structure Reforming

    The proposition of a total change in green capitalism lies in changing the process it is built upon. Technological and organizational fixes, such as cutting energy costs, improving packaging materials, minimizing transport distances, and building green brands credentials are some of the facets that drive green capitalism toward the capitalistic side rather than the green side.

Recommendations.

For the solutions proposed to solve air pollution and mitigate its effects to society, future solutions need to keep in mind that science should always be inclusive. It must always answer yes to the question of accessibility, scalability, and comprehensiveness.

the focus of these solutions should be proposing solutions that target corporations and not the average person in the first place. If the responsibility of solving climate change falls under the average people then the corporations who abuse the environment will only take advantage of these solutions.

As much as we have failed to heed the forewarnings of science, there is still time for us to believe in science and solve these issues.
